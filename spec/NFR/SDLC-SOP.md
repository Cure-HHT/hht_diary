Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) SOPs
These are the most critical for an auditor. They prove the "how" and "why" of your code.
SDLC Management: How you plan, develop, and release software (e.g., your Agile/Scrum process mapped to GAMP 5).
Requirements Management: How you capture and hash requirements like REQ-CAL-p00029.
Code Review & Commit: The process for peer-reviewing code and ensuring only validated code reaches production.
Configuration Management: How you use Terraform, Git, and Doppler to control and track environment changes.
System Validation: How you perform OQ/PQ testing and generate your Traceability Matrix.
2. IT Infrastructure & Security SOPs
These prove that the GCP environment is secure and the data is protected.
User Access Control: How your team (developers/admins) gets access to the backend and how you perform periodic access reviews.
Data Backup & Recovery: Your automated backup schedule and—critically—proof that you have tested a Data Restoration.
Disaster Recovery (DR): What happens if a GCP region goes offline.
Encryption & Key Management: How you handle data at rest and in transit.
3. Quality & Compliance SOPs
These are the "corporate" procedures that ensure your company operates under a quality mindset.
Document Control: How you version-control your SOPs and PRDs (the "meta" process).
Training & Qualification: Proof that every dev has been trained on the SDLC and 21 CFR Part 11.
Incident & Bug Management: How you track, categorize (Critical/Major/Minor), and fix software bugs.
Change Control: A formal process for approving any change to a validated system.
CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action): How you investigate systemic failures and ensure they don't happen again.
4. Human Resources (The "Who")
Job Descriptions: Every role (e.g., Lead Developer, QA Engineer) must have a signed job description.
CV/Resume Management: Updated and signed resumes for all staff involved in the project.
Summary Checklist for Your Binder
SOP Name    Why the Sponsor Needs It
SDLC SOP    To verify the software was built under control.
Change Control SOP    To ensure updates won't break their study data.
Backup & DR SOP    To satisfy the "Data Retention" requirement.
Training SOP    To prove the devs aren't "amateurs" in clinical tech.
Access Management SOP    To verify Authority Checks (§11.10(g)).
Pro-Tip: You don't necessarily have to give them the full text of every SOP upfront. Most vendors provide a SOP Index (a list of titles and version numbers) and then let the sponsor review specific ones during a live screen-share or "Read-Only" portal access.
